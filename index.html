A Pitch for Non-cooperative Games

Positive feedback loops are essential to superintelligence, and non-cooperative games can produce one of the strongest intelligence-amplifying positive feedback loops when (1) they exert "intelligence pressure" on players and (2) the players learn.

I believe we've already seen early signs of this potential in GANs and self-play. But fundamental questions remain open: how and when do multiple neural networks converge to their (local) equilibrium, and what is the nature of such an equilibrium?

R1: Stochastic Gradient Methods in Smooth Games

In smooth games, each player tries to optimize its own smooth objective using gradients. The challenge is that competing objectives often induce non-convergent cycles. In fact, both Nash equilibria and stationary points are computationally intractable in general. So the theory typically splits into two categories: local analysis under a general setup and global analysis under structural assumptions. My first AISTATS 2022 paper studies the former and my recent COLT 2026 paper studies the latter. I'm working on a follow-up paper toward answering a more fundamental question: "what's the weakest computationally tractable structure for smooth games?"

R2: Independent Learning Methods in Markov Games

In real-world applications, we seldom enjoy gradient feedback, and we often only have access to stochastic rewards for each player. Markov games naturally model this setting, and independent learning is the most straightforward way to learn in Markov games. Classical zero-sum Markov games are now relatively well-established. But Markov games with general-sum, function approximation, and non-additive rewards are still in their infancy. Paralleling my work on smooth games, I'm planning to study the following question: "what's the weakest tractable structure for Markov games?"

R3: Implicit Equilibrium Selection Bias in Games

In the world of minimization, deep learning now has (at least) a partially satisfactory explanation: neural nets exhibit multiple solutions, but stochastic gradient methods prefer particularly desirable ones. I believe an analogous explanation would be crucial for games, and I'm working on the question: "given a continuum of (local) equilibria or limit cycles, which one do stochastic gradient methods prefer?"
